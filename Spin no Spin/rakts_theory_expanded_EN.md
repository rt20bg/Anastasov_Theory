Furthermore, before a particle's spin is measured, it exists in a ghostly "superposition"—pointing both up and down simultaneously. When it enters a magnetic field, it allegedly undergoes a mystical, instantaneous "collapse," randomly choosing to become either entirely "spin-up" or "spin-down."

## 3. Deconstructing the Origin: The Stern-Gerlach Experiment
In the famous 1922 Stern-Gerlach experiment, a beam of random silver atoms passed through two magnets creating a flat vertical gradient (a dipole magnet). The beam cleanly split into two distinct lines on the detector screen. Copenhagen physicists pointed to this and declared: *"See! The abstract spin collapsed randomly into two distinct states!"*

RAKTS explains this without quantum mysticism:
1. The atom enters the apparatus with a random spatial dipole vector.
2. The massive magnetic pressure violently forces the dipole to quickly rotate and snap into the vertical axis—some pointing fully North, others fully South, depending on which pole they were already slightly leaning towards.
3. Once snapped into a stable alignment, classical electrodynamic force—calculated simply as exactly $$F = \nabla(\vec{\mu} \cdot \vec{B})$$—drags the particle continuously either up or down. 
There is no superposition. There is only high-speed alignment followed by classical deflection.

Mainstream physics often ignores experimental realities that contradict its statistical models. RAKTS draws upon undisputed experimental data to prove that "spin" behavior is purely geometric and spatial.

This behavior is 100% reliant on the physical, spatial geometry of the magnetic gradient. The particles are not generating random numbers; they are kinematic objects flowing along spatial pressure gradients.

### Evidence B: Majorana Spin-Flip and the Ioffe-Pritchard Trap
In the dead center of the 4-magnet quadrupole, the magnetic field reaches an absolute value of Zero ($B=0$). 
When particles pass perfectly through this dead center, something catastrophic happens in the lab: the particles violently "forget" their alignment, undergo chaotic flipping (known in atomic physics as the *Majorana Spin-Flip*), and are aggressively ejected from the trap center.
Why? Because without a background magnetic field to provide an orientation axis, the particle loses its "compass". To fix this, physicists in the 1990s introduced a "bias field"—an intentional imbalance in the magnets (the *Ioffe-Pritchard Trap*). By ensuring the field at the center never hit absolute zero, the particles always had a physical field to orient against, keeping them stable.
